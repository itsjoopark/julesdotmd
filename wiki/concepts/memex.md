# Memex

## Definition

A hypothetical device proposed by Vannevar Bush in his 1945 essay "As We May Think." The Memex (memory extender) was envisioned as a personal, mechanized knowledge store where an individual could store all their books, records, and communications, and retrieve them with speed and flexibility through associative trails — links between documents that the user creates as they browse.

## Key Points

- Bush's vision was **private and actively curated** — closer to a personal wiki than to the public web that eventually emerged
- The most important feature was **associative trails**: user-created connections between documents, where the connections themselves are as valuable as the documents
- The Memex was never built — it was a conceptual vision published in The Atlantic Monthly
- Bush's unsolved problem was **who does the maintenance**: creating and keeping associative trails current required continuous human effort
- The [[LLM Wiki]] pattern explicitly claims to solve Bush's maintenance problem — LLMs handle the cross-referencing and bookkeeping that make a Memex-like system sustainable

## Examples

- The Memex was imagined as a desk with translucent screens, a keyboard, and microfilm storage
- A researcher would build "trails" through their library, linking related articles and annotating connections — essentially manual hyperlinking decades before the web

## Connections

- [[Second Brain]] — the modern descendant of the Memex idea
- [[RAG]] — an alternative approach that retrieves at query time rather than building persistent trails
